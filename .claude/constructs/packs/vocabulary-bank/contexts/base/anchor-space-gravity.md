# Anchor. Space. Gravity.

> The design philosophy underneath the vocabulary bank. Three moves for meaning emergence.

**Source**: K-Hole deep research (2026-03-12), 452 grounded sources across game studios (Riot/Bungie/Supergiant), DeFi protocols (Aave/Uniswap/Stripe), crypto games (Axie/Loot/Dark Forest), and MMO economies (RuneScape/L2/EVE).

---

## The Principle

You can design the anchor points, but you cannot design the meaning. You can only design the conditions under which meaning emerges. Good anchors (material, consistent, open) expand the space for meaning. Bad anchors (abstract, prescriptive, noisy) collapse it.

---

## Move 1: Anchor

Ground words in material reality.

"Weight" not "position." "Compartment" not "account." "Key" not "token." Every word connects to something you can feel in your hands or see in a room. Physical anchors give people's imaginations a surface to grip. Abstract words slide off the mind. Material words stick.

**The test**: Can someone who has never used your product picture what this word means?

**What anchoring is NOT**: renaming everything. Tier 1 terms (deposit, withdraw, approve, failed) stay operational precisely because they ARE the anchors the user brings from the bazaar. You don't rename the shared language -- you add physical vocabulary alongside it.

### References

- **Destiny's "engram"**: A made-up word anchored in a physical object (a glowing polyhedron). Nobody says "I got a loot drop." They say "I got an engram."
- **RuneScape's "Grand Exchange"**: Not "marketplace." A specific place you walk to. The name carries the weight of the building.
- **Dark Souls' "bonfire"**: Not "checkpoint." A fire you sit at. The anchor is warmth in darkness.

---

## Move 2: Space

Don't fill the gap between the anchors.

"Compartment closed. Honeycomb returned." gives you two anchors and trusts you to feel what that means. "The vault appreciates your counsel" fills the space with prescribed meaning, which is always less valuable than the meaning someone builds themselves.

**The test**: After writing a piece of copy, can you remove a word without losing the operational signal? If yes, remove it. The words you didn't write are doing more work than the words you did.

### The spectrum

| Too empty | Right amount | Too full |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| "Done." | "Compartment closed. Honeycomb returned." | "Congratulations! Your vault compartment has been successfully closed and your Honeycomb NFT has been returned to your wallet!" |
| (No anchors to grip) | (Two anchors, space between) | (Every gap filled, nothing left to build) |

### Where space is safe and where it isn't

| Moment | Space tolerance |
|--------|---------------|
| Discovery | High -- users have cognitive surplus, exploring |
| Idle/Ambient | High -- users are absorbing atmosphere |
| Action | Medium -- frame with L2, act with L1 |
| Confirmation | Low -- operational signal must be immediate and unambiguous |
| Error | Lowest -- what happened, what to do, nothing else |

---

## Move 3: Gravity

Use the anchors consistently until they accumulate meaning beyond their definition.

"Weight" starts as a label on day one. By day thirty it's a concept. By day ninety it's a feeling. You never said any of that. The user built it through repeated contact with a word that held its ground.

**The test**: If you changed this word to its operational synonym, would users notice and feel a loss? If yes, the word has accumulated gravity. If no, it hasn't earned its place yet.

### The accumulation timeline

```
Tier 3 (Reserved)     -> word exists in worldbuilding, NOT in UI
                         | deploy in discovery moments
Tier 2 (Earned)       -> word used consistently in discovery + idle
                         | migrate to action contexts
Tier 2 (Established)  -> word appears in action + confirmation moments
                         | time + consistency
Gravity               -> word carries meaning beyond its definition
```

### References

- **Destiny's "engram"**: 10 years of consistent use in one context (loot reveal). Now carries genuine emotional weight.
- **EVE's "PLEX"**: CCP introduced a formal term. Players shortened it to "PLEXing." The formal term had enough gravity that the shorthand inherited its meaning.
- **RuneScape's "bank"**: Just "bank." 20 years. Now carries the entire weight of inventory management, trading prep, nostalgia, and community identity.

---

## The Three Moves Together

| Decision | Move | Why |
|----------|------|-----|
| Use material words over abstractions | Anchor | Physical metaphor, surface to grip |
| Two anchors with space between | Space | Trust the user to build meaning |
| Tier promotion through consistent exposure | Gravity | Words earn their place over time |
| Don't anthropomorphize systems | Space | Prescribed emotion fills the gap |
| Register ceiling matches context | Space | Surrounding world determines available space |
| Aviation copy for transaction states | Anchor | Short, physical, unambiguous |
| Consequence tooltips over definitions | Space + Gravity | Anchor and leave space; repetition adds gravity |

### How to use this

When writing or auditing copy, ask three questions in order:

1. **Anchor**: Is every word grounded in something material or operational? Could someone picture it?
2. **Space**: Am I telling the user what to feel, or am I giving them anchors and letting them build?
3. **Gravity**: Has this word earned its place through prior exposure, or am I deploying it cold?

If a word fails any of these tests, it's either the wrong word (Anchor), too much word (Space), or the right word at the wrong time (Gravity).

---

## What this model is NOT

- **It is not "use fancy words."** Tier 1 operational vocabulary is anchored too. The bazaar baseline IS an anchor system.
- **It is not "be mysterious."** Space means trusting the user to build meaning, not withholding information they need.
- **It is not "never change anything."** Gravity is earned, not permanent. If a word stops serving the product, changing it is correct even though it costs accumulated meaning.
